29-year-old spent $20,000 to open a store in New York — now it brings in $1.6 million a year

Based on the available description… Abby Price started Abbode as a Parsons grad student, selling dried floral arrangements on Facebook in 2019 and expanded to a Nolita storefront by March 2022. Strong shop cashflow let her buy a $15,000 embroidery machine on a whim, and for a year a key element of her business sat covered in dust in the store basement.

Key points
  • Founded Abbode while a Parsons student; began selling dried florals on Facebook in 2019.
  • Opened a physical storefront in Nolita by March 2022 selling bouquets and home decor.
  • Business cashflow funded a $15,000 embroidery machine purchase.
  • Price describes the equipment purchase as made "on a total whim."
  • A crucial part of the business sat unused in the shop basement for a year.
So what
  • On-site production equipment (e.g., a $15,000 embroidery machine) can introduce new localized electrical loads for small retailers.
  • Rapid growth from online sales to physical storefronts may create sudden, unpredictable capacity needs in urban areas.
  • Reliable power and flexible capacity planning support small businesses that add manufacturing or equipment-driven services.
  • Unused or intermittently used equipment can become an operational risk if brought online without advance infrastructure checks.
