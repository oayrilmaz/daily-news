Midday solar valleys and evening ramps reshape operating risk

Operators face a growing pattern: strong midday solar output that suppresses net load followed by steep late-afternoon ramps as solar falls and cooling demand peaks, creating thermal and stability pressure points across transmission corridors.

Signals from recent operating windows point to deeper mid-day net-load troughs in high-penetration regions and sharper ramps later in the day. This pattern compresses flexibility needs into shorter time windows and raises reliance on fast-ramping assets and storage dispatch strategies. Thermal ratings on key transmission elements may be strained by reverse flows and localized congestion as distributed PV exports push power into constrained corridors. Voltage and reactive-power management complexity grows when large inverters shift from export to absorption modes through the sunset transition. Reserve scheduling practices built on smoother diurnal curves may miss the new ramp timing, increasing the odds of manual interventions. Operators should consider scenario-driven capacity overlays and re-evaluate contingency plans that assume slower, predictable ramps.

What to watch
  • Late-afternoon net-load trajectories in major balancing areas
  • Unusual reverse-flow alarms on transmission feeders
Action

Run focused ramp and thermal contingency scenarios for next-week evening peak periods and validate fast-ramp resource readiness.
