Draft reliability standards are creating interconnection timing uncertainty

Draft reliability proposals are signaling new interconnection and planning requirements that could lengthen project timelines and shift cost allocation in transmission expansion cases.

Regulatory drafts and stakeholder responses are producing signals that reliability standards will emphasize coordinated planning and resilience metrics. This reframes interconnection expectations for developers and transmission owners. The intelligence framing highlights that new studies may require more granular modeling of distributed resources and converter-based controls. Project sponsors should expect longer study windows and potentially new mitigation obligations. Cost allocation signals are uncertain. If standards mandate more proactive reinforcement, the resulting financing and tariff constructs could reallocate costs across ratepayers and interconnection customers. Operational compliance requirements—such as mandatory grid-forming inverter capabilities or enhanced telemetry—are being discussed and could raise equipment specification costs. Policy timelines remain a critical watchpoint. The window between draft issuance and final rule adoption will determine whether current projects are grandfathered or subject to retroactive requirements. Scenario watch: a final reliability standard that tightens interconnection tests could push large renewable or storage projects into extended queue phases, creating scheduling and financing stress.

What to watch
  • Finalization timelines for draft reliability standards and grandfathering provisions.
  • Proposed technical requirements for inverter-based resources and telemetry.
Action

Map project timelines against draft-to-final rule windows and prepare compliance cost estimates for proposed technical upgrades.
