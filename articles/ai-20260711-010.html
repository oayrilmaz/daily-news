Contract risk rises as weather-related force majeure scenarios grow

Operational signals point to an uptick in weather-related disruptions that are prompting EPCs and owners to reassess contractual protections and project contingency sizing.

In regions experiencing more frequent extreme weather events, force majeure clauses and allocation of weather risk are being renegotiated or litigated more often. That trend increases timeline and cost uncertainty for projects.

EPCs are pricing contingencies into bids and seeking stronger pass-through mechanisms for weather delays. Owners are pushing back and requesting firm performance commitments, creating transactional friction.

Insurance markets are responding by tightening coverage terms or raising premiums for weather-exposed projects. That movement reallocates risk between private insurers and capital providers and can slow down financing timelines.

Project schedules are being reworked to include larger buffer windows and more robust temporary works strategies to protect critical-path components. These tactics add upfront cost but can reduce schedule slip risk.

Contract structures that incentivize resilience investments or share upside for early completion are emerging as practical scenarios to align interests under higher weather volatility.

Stakeholders should expect more negotiation over site access, temporary works, and acceptance testing standards that account for weather-driven execution variability.

What to watch
  • Insurance premium movements and claims trends in weather-impacted regions.
  • Contract amendment patterns and dispute notices tied to weather events.
Action

Review contract language for weather risk allocation and ensure contingencies reflect updated climate and schedule exposure.
