Substation automation investments highlight cybersecurity and interoperability trade-offs

As utilities accelerate substation automation to improve resilience and reduce operating costs, interoperability gaps and cybersecurity hygiene are becoming central operational concerns.

Deployments of IEC 61850 and similar standards are expanding, but variations in implementation create interoperability friction between vendor equipment. That friction raises commissioning time and integration costs. Cybersecurity teams stress the need for segmentation, signed firmware, and consistent patching processes. Applying these controls at scale in brownfield deployments is operationally challenging. Some operators are piloting secure gateways and protocol translation layers to insulate legacy equipment while enabling advanced automation functions. Those gateways introduce an additional layer to validate and maintain. Training and organizational capability are proving as important as technology choices; workforce upskilling in OT cybersecurity and standards compliance is a recurring signal from pilots. In a scenario where interoperability or security issues delay automation benefits, expected cost savings and reliability gains could be deferred, affecting ROI calculations. What to monitor: announcements of automation frameworks, vendor interoperability testbeds, and insurer guidance on OT cyber hygiene. Operators should prioritize segmentation and a clear firmware governance model when planning automation rollouts.

What to watch
  • vendor interoperability test results
  • insurer or regulator guidance on OT cybersecurity
Action

Adopt strict segmentation and a firmware governance plan before scaling automation in brownfield substations.
