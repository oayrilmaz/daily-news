Interconnection and queue reform discussions intensify in regulatory corridors

Regulatory discussions are signaling movement toward queue reform and faster interconnection processes to reduce project churn and better reflect network constraints in procurement timelines.

Policymakers and regulators are increasingly focused on reducing speculative projects in interconnection queues that block capacity for firm developers. Reforms under consideration aim to improve project attrition signals and financial commitment rules. Faster interconnection could accelerate renewables and storage deployment but requires upstream transmission planning and rights-of-way reforms that are politically sensitive and time-consuming. Some jurisdictions are piloting 'connect-and-manage' frameworks that allow generation to connect earlier under operating constraints. These pilots provide operational learning but shift curtailment and curtailment-compensation dynamics. Regulatory reforms also consider stronger coordination between transmission planning and local permitting to shorten timelines. That introduces trade-offs between speed and local stakeholder engagement. Market participants are revising underwriting assumptions for new projects in anticipation of changing queue rules and potential prioritization metrics based on system value rather than first-come status. Scenario watch: rapid rule adoption without commensurate transmission funding would likely increase operational curtailment and developer disputes; balanced implementation and interim compensation mechanisms are key.

What to watch
  • Regulatory filings on queue reform and connect-and-manage pilots
  • Transmission funding allocations and planning milestone announcements
Action

Monitor rule changes and reassess project underwriting and interconnection strategies to reflect anticipated queue reforms.
