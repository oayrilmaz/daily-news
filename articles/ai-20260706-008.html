Project delivery models are shifting as EPCs adapt to workforce and modularization pressures

Signals from project planning circles point to greater reliance on modular assembly and workforce localization in the Middle East, reshaping EPC risk allocation and schedule predictability.

EPC firms are increasingly pitching modularized HV equipment and prefabricated substation skids to compress on-site construction timelines. This reduces some field labor needs but requires earlier design freeze. Workforce localization policies are prompting EPCs to invest more in local training and staffing pipelines. This increases near-term costs but is positioned as a long-term resiliency play. Logistics strategies are being revisited as ports and inland transport capacity determine the viability of large prefabricated modules. Early engagement with logistics partners is becoming standard practice. Contract terms are adjusting to reflect these shifts, with more emphasis on scope clarity, factory witness testing, and supplier delivery assurances to manage modular risks. There is an expectation that modular approaches will improve schedule reliability but may shift certain technical risks upstream into the procurement and factory-acceptance phases. Clients and financiers are being advised to include acceptance-testing milestones and contingency allowances for import and customs delays in project budgets. What to monitor: modular prefab acceptance testing protocols, changes to local workforce regulations, and port or transport capacity updates affecting large module movements.

What to watch
  • Factory acceptance and witness-testing schedules for prefabricated modules
  • Local workforce regulation changes and port capacity announcements
Action

Incorporate factory-acceptance milestones and logistics contingency in contracts and sponsor local workforce training programs.
