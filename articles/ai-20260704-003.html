Second-life batteries and fire-safety signals raise integration questions for data centers

Signals from pilots and design discussions indicate second-life EV batteries are attractive for cost and sustainability reasons, but thermal risk and standards gaps could complicate integration in critical facilities.

Interest in second-life battery packs for data-center UPS and peak-shaving is increasing as capex pressures mount. Early deployments highlight cost advantages but also variability in cell health distribution within repurposed packs. Thermal management and fire-detection systems designed for new batteries may not fully account for the degradation characteristics of second-life systems. Certification frameworks for large-format second-life energy storage in critical facilities are still evolving and may not align with uptime requirements. Unexpected thermal events in other sectors point to the need for enhanced BMS, segregation strategies, and dedicated suppression approaches in data centers. Operators should run scenario analyses that include failure modes unique to second-life modules and plan redundancy accordingly. Procurement teams will need to qualify vendors on both performance and safety management processes before scale-up. Regulators and insurers may take a cautious stance, potentially affecting project timelines and operating costs.

What to watch
  • monitor thermal runaway incident reports and certification updates for second-life systems
  • track insurer and regulator guidance on repurposed EV batteries in critical facilities
Action

Require vendor safety dossiers and run thermal-failure simulations before accepting second-life battery proposals.
