AI models for predictive maintenance: adoption trade-offs and operational dependency

Emerging deployments of AI-driven predictive maintenance are producing signals of lower unplanned outages but also raising scenario questions about operational dependency, model drift, and vendor lock-in for critical HV equipment.

AI models trained on fleet telemetry can surface failure precursors earlier than threshold-based alarms, creating potential operational gains in maintenance planning and spare inventory management. However, model performance varies by equipment vintage, OEM telemetry fidelity, and condition-labeling practices. This heterogeneity increases the risk of overconfidence in model-derived recommendations without proper validation. Model drift is a realistic operational concern as equipment populations change or as control strategies evolve. Continuous retraining and domain-expert oversight are necessary to maintain meaningful performance over time. Integration of AI outputs into maintenance workflows requires cultural and procedural changes; technicians and engineers must trust model signals and have clear escalation and verification protocols. Dependence on vendor-supplied models raises contractual and data-ownership questions. Organizations should evaluate portability and explainability of models to avoid vendor lock-in and to ensure auditability during incident reviews. Adopting AI for predictive maintenance is promising but should be staged, with robust validation, human-in-the-loop checks, and contracts that preserve model access and retraining rights.

What to watch
  • Performance validation reports from AI pilot deployments
  • Contract terms regarding model access, retraining, and data portability
Action

Run controlled AI maintenance pilots with human oversight and require contractual data/model portability clauses.
