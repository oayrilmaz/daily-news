Curtailment risk rising as inertia drops in high-renewable grids

Grid areas with rapid renewable buildouts show signals of growing curtailment risk and non-synchronous stability constraints, pointing to the near-term need for grid-forming resources and revised dispatch rules.

High penetrations of inverter-based resources reduce system inertia and complicate frequency response in large disturbances. Operational mitigations such as minimum synchronous generation commitments increase operating cost and can create market friction. Curtailment economics will influence project revenues and could slow investment if persistent patterns emerge without mitigation incentives. Grid-forming inverters and fast frequency response technologies offer technical pathways but require regulatory and operational acceptance. Transmission bottlenecks and constrained interconnection processes amplify local curtailment even when aggregate system capacity exists. What to monitor are frequency event rates, constrained-area curtailment levels, and procurement signals for grid-forming equipment or fast reserves. Scenario watch: prolonged periods of low system inertia could necessitate temporary curtailment or mandatory synchronous unit dispatch to preserve reliability.

What to watch
  • frequency disturbance frequency and depth over next 72h
  • procurement notices for grid-forming inverters or fast reserves
Action

Assess exposure to curtailment in interconnection queues and engage with operators on grid-forming resource plans.
