HV equipment corrosion and AC/DC conversion projects present localized reliability scenarios

Signals of accelerated AC-to-DC conversion projects and saline-corrosion exposure suggest targeted reliability interventions for high-voltage equipment in coastal and arid environments.

Coastal and desert substations face distinct degradation modes: salt-induced corrosion and thermal stress respectively. The intelligence framing is that conversion projects and retrofits must factor in localized environmental stressors.

Scenarios include the prioritized replacement of corroded switchgear and the adoption of corrosion-resistant coatings and enclosure designs. Another scenario is increased use of DC-linked transmission for long-distance links, changing substation equipment mixes.

EPCs and OEMs may need to adjust specifications to incorporate environmental hardening, which can increase unit costs and extend lead times. Spare-part strategies must also account for accelerated wear in these contexts.

For asset managers, lifecycle models calibrated on temperate climates will understate replacement rates in harsh environments. Adjusted inspection cadences and predictive analytics tuned to local conditions are prudent.

Deployment of DC configurations introduces interoperability questions for protection schemes and grounding practices. These require validated testing protocols before operational handover.

Monitor procurement specs for hardened equipment and any pilot results from AC-to-DC conversion projects as indicators of broader adoption.

What to watch
  • Requests for proposals that specify corrosion-resistant materials.
  • Pilot project outcomes for AC-to-DC conversion at transmission scale.
Action

Adjust inspection schedules and update specifications to reflect environmental hardening needs for HV equipment.
